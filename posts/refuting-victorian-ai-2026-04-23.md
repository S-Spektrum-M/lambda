# The Manure Problem: Why AI Energy Consumption Isn't the Crisis You Think It Is

In 1894, The Times of London estimated that by 1950, every street in the city would be buried under nine feet of horse manure. The projection was mathematically sound. The population was growing, horses were the dominant mode of transport, and each one produced 15–30 pounds of manure per day. The crisis was real, imminent, and unsolvable within the existing technological paradigm.

Then the car showed up. Not because anyone was trying to solve the manure problem --- but because the car was simply, objectively, a better technology. People adopted it because it was faster, more versatile, and cheaper at scale. The manure crisis didn't get solved. It got made irrelevant.

I think the current panic over AI-driven energy consumption is the same story, and we're playing the role of the Victorians staring at the manure projections.

## The Argument

The standard framing goes like this:
- AI training and inference require enormous compute.
- Compute requires energy.
- Energy production contributes to climate change.
- Therefore AI accelerates climate change.

Each step in isolation is true. The conclusion is wrong in any meaningful timesacle because it treats the energy
landscape as static.

## The Haber Precedent

Consider the Haber process. In 1909, Fritz Haber figured out how to pull nitrogen from the atmosphere and turn it into
ammonia. Industrial fertilizer followed. Roughly half the people alive today owe their existence to this single
invention.

The environmental costs were severe: nitrogen runoff creating ocean dead zones, nitrous oxide emissions, massive natural gas consumption. These are real, ongoing problems.

But we've been iterating. Precision agriculture reduces runoff. Nitrification inhibitors cut emissions. Green hydrogen is beginning to replace natural gas as the energy input. And biological nitrogen fixation --- engineering crops to do what the Haber process does industrially --- is an active area of research that could eventually make the industrial process unnecessary for agriculture entirely.

Nobody would seriously argue that we should have skipped the Haber process and let billions starve to avoid nitrogen runoff. The technology created problems, and then subsequent technology addressed those problems. The trajectory matters more than any single snapshot.

## The Real Bottleneck Is Adoption, Not Innovation

Here's the thing that doesn't get said enough: the climate problem isn't fundamentally a technology problem. It's an adoption problem.

France went majority nuclear in the 1970s. Their grid is among the cleanest in Europe. The technology was available to everyone. The United States and Germany chose differently --- not because of engineering limitations, but because of politics, NIMBYism, and a strain of environmentalism that paradoxically treated one of the cleanest energy sources as an existential threat.

Solar is now the cheapest source of electricity in most markets on Earth. The technology isn't hypothetical. It exists, it's proven, and it's economically superior. The gap between where we are and where we need to be isn't an innovation gap. It's the friction of political will, regulatory inertia, and sunk costs in legacy infrastructure.

## AI as Accelerant

This reframes the AI energy question entirely.

The concern is that AI consumes energy. The reframe: AI dramatically increases demand for cheap, abundant energy, which accelerates the economic case for nuclear and solar, which accelerates the transition we need to make anyway.

Datacenters don't want to pay for expensive, unreliable energy. They want cheap, dense, 24/7 power. That's a description of nuclear. The hyperscalers are already investing in small modular reactors and signing long-term power purchase agreements with nuclear operators. AI isn't pulling us away from clean energy --- it's creating the demand signal that justifies the capital expenditure.

In the short term, yes, some of that compute runs on natural gas. In the medium term, the economic pressure points toward exactly the energy mix the climate needs.

## Who Actually Pays for the Regression?

Would a child in Monterrey prefer his parents buy him food, or that algal blooms in the Gulf of Mexico didn't exist?

That's the Haber tradeoff in one sentence. The nitrogen runoff is real. The dead zone is real. But the fertilizer that caused it is also the reason that child eats. You don't get to undo one without undoing the other, and the people who bear the cost of undoing it are never the ones making the argument.

The same applies here. Restricting AI in the name of climate sounds principled in the abstract. But who actually absorbs the regression? Technology has been relentlessly democratizing --- the internet took distribution out of the hands of publishers, and AI is doing something similar right now: it lets a small business owner operate without absorbing the overhead of full-time staff, making a three-person shop competitive with an enterprise incumbent in ways that were previously impossible.

Banning or heavily restricting AI in the name of climate doesn't distribute the pain evenly. It preserves the advantages of organizations that already have scale, capital, and headcount, and pulls the ladder up behind them. The environmental argument becomes a vehicle for incumbent protection --- probably unintentionally, but the effect is the same.

The people calling loudest for AI restrictions are rarely the ones whose livelihoods depend on the access it provides.

## The Faith Argument

Underlying all of this is a question about whether you trust future generations to solve problems that current technology creates. I do. Not blindly --- but based on the observable pattern that people adopt objectively better technology, and that the pace of technological improvement is accelerating.

Every generation inherits problems from the last one. Every generation also inherits better tools. The bet that AI-accelerated innovation outpaces the environmental cost of running it isn't naive optimism. It's the same bet that has paid off at every previous inflection point in technological history.

The Victorians could have banned horses to prevent the manure crisis. They didn't need to. The car was coming.

The clean energy transition is coming too. The only question is whether AI gets there faster, or whether we slow both down together.
